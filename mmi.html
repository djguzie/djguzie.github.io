Summary

This was my first contracted UX opportunity for a client. Finally a real product that actually exists! I worked with the design team at Shion Studio to revamp, modernize and organize content for an ad-fraud detection company. The final version of their site is light-years ahead of were they used to be. It's modern, a delight to use, and most importantly easier to navigate. As a bonus, I also worked to deploy this site and made some final tweaks to make sure it was ready to launch.

Problem

Advertising is big business and when bots trick advertisters into thinking they have millions of views when its really only thousands, that hurts a company's bottom line. Given MMI has such a powerful product, their website did not reflect this. Their site was wordy, hard to navigate and had no design concept. My task was to reorganize the content and streamline the experience by creating solid mockups. This was further complicated becuase the client did not employ in-house web developers and so they needed a site designed around Squarespace templates for easy use. Working within the limited templates of Squarespace was a big worry for Shion as this constraint was a new endeavor for them.

Solution

The final site gives Method Media Intelligence a much needed facelift entering this new decade. Their stiff and unintersting website now had a strong navy color to anchor softer shades of turquoise and baby blue. The most relevant items are on the navigation bar while less important details were moved to the footer. Nearly all of the copy was reworded and I made catchier headlines to pique people's interest. Long areas of text are shortened and broken up with impactful imagery and designs. More emphasis was placed on getting prospective clients to request a demo and so far there has been more interest in this area. The clients were quite happy with the site and I can't wait to show you how it all came together.

Process

UX Audit

Before I started the work of reorganizing content, I poked around and explored the current state of the MMI website. Based on my inital use, and my Shion Studio Design Manager's feedback, I created a list of the biggest usability issues to tackle in our revamp.

UX AUDIT TAKEAWAYS
  • The navigation bar has too many options making it difficult to focus on the important content
  • Some items from the navigation bar would benefit from being in a footer or being collapsed into somewhere else
  • Long sections of text could be improved by using strong imagery and grouping content better
  • The content itself could use some help to provide attention grabbing headlines that encourage discovery
  • The website had only one opportunity to scheudle a demo so an increase in calls to action would help to allow for MMI to capture more leads easily
  • The design of the site felt limp, and uninteresting and needs a more appealing, modern and business forward aesthetic
Sitemap

The sitemap I created from the current design helped me visually see places that could benefit from reorganizing. Key in this new version would be a footer, where secondary content like the GDPR and Privacy Policy can live. This also has the benefit of creating a clear and focused navigation which helps users focus on the content. In addition, I wanted more opportunities for users to request information and set up demos with MMI. Nearly every page in the sitemap got a facelift, except for the FAQ which didn't change much. Color & Typography

Creating a bold, gamer-centered marketplace I decided on a mostly black interface with a monochrome palette to give the app a modern, sleek feel. But if GameChanger was going to be bold, I needed a bold color. I chose red as its signature color because it demands attention, without doing much. If I‚Äôm being honest, it‚Äôs also a subtle homage to Nintendo, which uses red as its main color and many of my users told me they supported Nintendo as a company more than any other brand. Varying shades of red were also chosen to support the monochromatic color scheme.

I have been looking for ways to find a good serif font as a headline and thought this may be a good time to try. I eventually narrowed down my choices to 4 options each with a unique contrasting typeface. Usability Hub helped me create a user test but I had no clear winner for a favorite so I decided to choose the typeface that best represented GameChanger- bold and slightly playful. The ligatures of Raleway made me think of a serif font, but it was still a sans serif with a slight boldness to it. I decided to stick to one family because font pairing seemed too forced when I tried a few combonations.

What I've Learned

Looking back at the original problem I think GameChanger would be a worthy app to help gamers find what they want for no money out of pocket. I know that an app with no clear path to build revenue seems very idealistic, but some of the biggest apps of today started like that (Twitter and Facebook come to mind). They found ways to monetize only after they had millions of users who wanted their product. Thinking about it that way, users could potentially pay to spotlight their trade items or be alerted when particular items are available. It would be an easy way to monetize a part of the experience, completely optional, while maintaining a free core user experience for everyone else.

If I had more time I would like to build out a few screens of how to verify a user. Related to this, it would be nice to show a few screens on how someone would rate a user after a successful transaction. Verification was one of the most important features for users according to survey responses. I think I showed where I was going with that idea, but given more time I could show exactly what that would look like. Building a community takes dedicated members and all the big marketplace apps have some form of review or validation. I know that with those screens built, I could convey the important community aspects of the platform and help build a safe and trustworthy space.
