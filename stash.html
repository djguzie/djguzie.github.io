Stash

Cloud storage is boring. Stash makes it fun.

Summary

Today memes, gifs, and funny videos, are their own unique form of communication. And while Silicon Valley heatedly exchanges spreadsheets and documents for the big meeting, millions of other people share a silly picture becuase it's Throwback Thursday. The goal of this project is to bridge the usability and ease of cloud storage with all the intersting ways people communicate.

Problem

Through my survey research and interviews, I found that some people found their cloud storage options daunting. They associated these apps as more "businessy". They also didn't think they could see a benefit from them since they didn't use file storage daily. When I dove a bit deeper I found that they did in fact use and send files daily, but they didn't consider memes or gifs "files". They stockpiled them in their text conversations, photo library and social media, but these places made it hard to find the right file when they needed it the most.

Solution

Enter Stash.

Stash was created to store and organize the files people share and store the most- pictures, gifs, memes, videos or anything else they care about. It's easy to use, approachable and never takes itself too seriously. It organizes content automatically or allows users to do it for themselves. It's a modern solution for the even more modern problem of having too much stuff.

Process

User Surveys

I created a user survey to determine what frustrates cloud storage users and what they enjoy about the service. I also wanted to see if there was something I could salvage from their current offerings, while potentially discovering an unmet need.

Survey Data
Survey Highlights
  • 60% of surveyed users state “Easy to Use” as their top reason for using their cloud storage app
  • 83% of users value “Uploading Files” and “Category Organization” as the most important feature for cloud storage
  • “Content Creation” ranked last in features users would enjoy
  • Almost half of survey partcipants state they hardly use cloud storage outside of work
  • Nearly one fourth of participants stated they didn't use cloud storage at all

Competetive Analysis

The competitive analysis was also instrumental in uncovering opportunities for my cloud storage application. Dropbox is too enterprise focused, Google Drive has privacy concerns and Pinterest although simple to use, does not support all file types. Using my competitive analysis along with user survey data, I was able to combine the best features of existing cloud storage, address their opportunities and create an ideal solution: an approachable, easy to use application that helped users categorize and upload their files. Until this point I still believed I was creating a humdrum file storage application… but this would soon change.

User Personas

Now that my app had a series of broad features, it was time to create some User Personas. Synthesizing this raw data helps humanize the information into my potential audience for Stash.

Chloe

Age: 22 / Single / Retail Specialist San Francisco, CA

Motivations

Chloe lives on her mobile device and relies on it for constant communication with her friends. She loves Instagram and Snapchat but stays away from iCloud. She often communicates with images, gifs and memes with her friends that she saves from the internet. But when it comes time to reply, sometimes she can’t find the right file and ends up downloading or screenshotting the same content over again. She recieves daily messages about her storage being almost full.

Frustrations

Chloe is stressed from the daily chore of making space in her phone because she takes too many screenshots. She is also weary of storage applications. Often she is forgetful of where a file is becuase she has to look in many places to find it.

“I have so much stuff I don't know where any of it is”
Raymond

Age: 29 / Single / Marketing Manager Oakland, CA

Motivations

Raymond is a marketing manager who leads a very busy life. He has a team which he manages, and relies on them heavily to keep tasks organized. With no time to cook, he is a big fan of the convenience of food delivery apps and orders it several times a week. He would love to date more, but given his time commitments in other areas and his career aspirations, he doesn’t currently have the bandwidth.

Frustrations

Swamped with too many time commitments files pile up quickly for Raymond. He ends up spending too much time looking for files and organizing things. He wishes that his files could be organized for him in some way

“It would be amazing to get some time back and have things work automatically”
User Flows & User Stories

Once the personas were fleshed out, I created some user stories for first time and returning users to my app. I then mapped out user flows for some of the most pressing features like account on-boarding and uploading content.

View User Stories
Wireframes

Taking inventory of all the features, flows and personas I built a basic functioning version of the site. I wanted large page-wide images with a focus on simplicity. Once the wireframes were done, I made them clickable and created the first working version. I conducted remote and in-person user tests that helped shaped future iterations. One of the big takeaways from these tests was that the flow for sharing and collaborating was nearly identical and somewhat confusing. Humbled by the tests, I condensed that flow into one process that could be either shared or collaborated easily and wouldn’t confuse my users.

Branding & Identity

Once I hit the branding phase, the app really began to take shape. I started my branding sketches focusing on the sharing aspect and drew up many logo iterations. Since this was mainly focused on the word “cloud,” feedback from some peers and industry professionals urged me to focus on the storage aspect instead, since users seemed to gel with this concept more. I used a mind map, and a word association list and worked hard to create a name that reminded people how easy it can be to store things. Below, you can see how I ditched “Breeze Bin” and “Cinch Stash” and went with a much simpler name: Stash. Finally my app had a name!

With both designs side by side, I made the decision to go with concept #2 because it’s rounded font and drawer details delivered on the friendly easy-going vibe I wanted for the brand.

Small changes then were made to the logo to help tighten up the design. Below you can see slight changes to size and kerning of the font, spacing of the words relative to the drawer icon and shortening drawer width to match the length of the words. They may seem like tiny changes, but these litte details make a lasting impression that will represent the brand of this company for years to come.

To get a sense of certain aspects of the color, design, voice and typography I created a style guide. I took care to choose a primary color that was common in the cloud storage space like navy blue, but secondary colors that made it easygoing and fun. I took time to focus on the voice of Stash, as this is a pivotal piece of what makes Stash different than other cloud storage companies. While competitors seize an opportunity to focus on a concrete goal for file storage (think collaborating for an end goal, completing expense reports, etc..), Stash has a much smaller goal: a place to store the stuff users care about so that they can quickly find it and share it.

User Testing

While the idea of Stash was good, preference testing and honest feedback was essential in making big changes. Users told me when they were confused, when they enjoyed something or when I flat out missed the mark. Their feedback helped create significant changes.

User Test Highlights
  • Added colored navigation bar on homepage to create some variety to the mostly white design
  • Created more visually interesting graphics based on some users saying design was “too simple”
  • Described some key features of Stash on homepage because some users didn’t quite understand what Stash did
  • Shortened the size of the footer because it was quite large in comparison to the header
  • Changed the placement of some of the navigation links to put more focus on sign in and sign up buttons
  • Used color and placement more strategically to better highlight calls to action, error states and notifications
  • Made a less obtrusive notification banner that appeared at the top of the page instead of the middle of the page
  • Shrunk the sidebar and changed its color to maintain consitency across landing page and the dashboard
  • Created more ways for users to change how they view their content in the dashboard

Conclusion

I wanted to create an app that was boring, basic and was a clone of all the other cloud storage apps. I really did. Promise! But in the process of trying to doing that, I actually began to care about a real solution for the files that my users sent to each other.

The biggest inspiration came from my own life and realizing that my girlfriend although she said she did not need cloud storage used her photo library and social media accounts to compile funny images to send me. This process was cumbersome and she was not a daily user of any of the big cloud storage companies. Another big inspiration was social media as it seems that people share, screenshot and show each other files more often- when was the last time someone close to you sent you a spreadsheet that wasn’t work-related?

If given more time I’d like to be able to make some of the sharing aspects more ironed out. I focused a lot on the user persona of the tech-averse person, but there is plenty of room to add functionality for more robust sharing and collaboration. I also wanted to create a place for comments once content was shared with someone. I envisioned a quasi-social media space for files shared that could create and spark even more conversation and shares. However my biggest regret was not making this a mobile app. I have a few mockups for a homepage and sign in, but given the social aspect of sharing and how often people have their mobile devices with them, a fully-featured mobile version makes the most sense.

It was important to me to help people like my girlfriend and many others, who use their devices to interact with those they care about. While the client had specific ideas about collaboration, potential users I surveyed had very different ideas about what they wanted out of an app like this. I hope in the future I can help companies better understand their users so that the products fit the user, and not expecting the user to conform to how companies envision their product.
